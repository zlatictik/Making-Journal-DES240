## Understanding Data & Data Visualisation
Before this week, I mostly thought of data as numbers or statistics. However, this course introduces the idea that data can be personal, creative, and expressive.

From watching Giorgia Lupi’s TED Talk, I learned about “data humanism,” which is about making data more personal and meaningful rather than purely analytical. This changed how I approached the experiment, as I focused on representing everyday experiences and behaviours visually.

---

## Experiment 1: Data Drawings

### What Data I Chose
For this experiment, I chose to track:
- How each social interaction at work made me feel  
- Every moment I picked up my phone to check social media (screen time each day)  
- Every decision I put off or avoided  

I chose this data because it reflects both my emotional experiences and my habits, including distractions and procrastination. This made the data more personal and subjective, rather than just tracking something purely numerical.

---

### Process
I started by recording my data throughout the week:
- Noting each social interaction at work and how it made me feel (e.g. positive, neutral, negative)  
- Each time I checked my phone for social media  
- Moments where I avoided or delayed decisions  

This required being more aware of my behaviour and emotions during the day, especially recognising patterns in how interactions affected my mood.

For my visual approach:
- Social interactions were represented using shapes or colours to reflect different emotions  
- Phone usage was shown through repeated marks or symbols to show frequency  
- Avoided decisions were represented more abstractly, using irregular or broken shapes to reflect hesitation or incompletion

### Exploration & Iteration
During the process, I experimented with different ways to represent each type of data:
- Testing different shapes/colours to communicate emotional responses  
- Changing density and repetition to show phone usage frequency  
- Exploring abstract and fragmented visuals for avoided decisions  

Some ideas felt too complex or unclear, especially when combining emotional and behavioural data. I simplified parts of the design to make sure the visuals were still readable.

### Final Outcome
My final drawings balanced emotion and clarity:
- Social interactions were visually distinct, using only blue for positive interactions and red colour for negative, making emotional patterns easier to notice. Instead of smiley and sad faces, I used solid filled with full color diferent sized shapes to show the size/impact of social interactions: bigger the shape is the more impact/emotion it had on me. The smaller the shape is, less emotions I felt.   
- Phone usage formed dense repeated pattern such as different length horizontal lines, highlighting frequency as well as length of each phone check.
- Avoided decisions appeared as fragmented/ unfinished star symbols, reflecting inconsistency and hesitation.

This combination helped show both structured behaviours and more unpredictable emotional experiences.

## Reflection
This experiment helped me understand how data can be turned into something visual and expressive, especially when the data is personal. Tracking emotions and behaviours made the process more engaging, but also more challenging.

One of the main difficulties was representing subjective feelings clearly. I found that simple visual systems worked better than overly complicated ones. I also noticed patterns in my own habits, especially how often I check my phone and avoid certain decisions.

Overall, this experiment showed me that data visualisation is not just about accuracy, but also about communication and meaning.

---

## TED Talk Reflection
A key idea from Giorgia Lupi’s talk was that data should tell human stories, not just display information.

This influenced my approach by focusing on personal and emotional data rather than purely numerical data. It made the outcome feel more meaningful and reflective of real experiences.
